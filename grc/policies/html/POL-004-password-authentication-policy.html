1. Purpose

This policy establishes minimum requirements for passwords, passphrases, and authentication mechanisms used to access organizational systems. Compromised credentials represent the leading attack vector in data breaches. This policy ensures that authentication controls provide adequate protection proportional to the sensitivity of the systems and data being accessed.

This policy implements NIST SP 800-63B Digital Identity Guidelines and NIST SP 800-53 Rev 5 IA control family requirements.

2. Scope

This policy applies to all accounts used to access organizational systems, applications, databases, and network infrastructure, including standard user accounts, privileged accounts, service accounts, and shared accounts where permitted.

3. Governing NIST Controls

IA-5
Authenticator Management: The organization manages system authenticators by verifying the identity of users before distributing authenticators; establishing minimum password complexity; enforcing password change after compromise; prohibiting password reuse for a defined number of generations; and using only approved cryptographic mechanisms to protect authenticators.
IA-2(1)
MFA for Privileged Access: The information system implements multi-factor authentication for network access to privileged accounts. One authentication factor must be a hardware-based authenticator or cryptographic device.
AC-7
Unsuccessful Logon Attempts: The information system enforces a limit of 5 consecutive invalid logon attempts during a 15-minute period and automatically locks the account for 30 minutes, requiring administrator unlock for privileged accounts.

4. Password Requirements

4.1 Minimum Complexity Standards

All passwords shall meet the following minimum requirements, consistent with NIST SP 800-63B Section 5.1.1:

Minimum Length12 characters16 characters25 characters
Complexity3 of 4 character classesAll 4 character classesRandom — password manager generated
Maximum Age365 days90 days365 days (managed)
HistoryLast 10 passwords prohibitedLast 24 passwords prohibitedN/A — always unique
MFA RequiredFor remote access and emailAlways — no exceptionsCertificate-based preferred

The four character classes are: uppercase letters (A–Z), lowercase letters (a–z), digits (0–9), and special characters (!@#$%^&*). Passwords shall not consist of dictionary words, keyboard patterns (qwerty, 12345), or variations of the username or organization name.

4.2 Prohibited Passwords

The following passwords and password patterns are explicitly prohibited: any password appearing in known-breached password lists (e.g., Have I Been Pwned corpus); any variation of the organization name, system name, or service name; any password containing the user's name, username, or employee ID; sequential characters (abc, 123) or repeated characters (aaa, 111); default vendor-supplied passwords which must be changed upon first use without exception.

4.3 Passphrases

Passphrases — sequences of four or more random words — are encouraged as an alternative to complex passwords, provided they meet minimum length requirements. A passphrase such as "correct-horse-battery-staple" is cryptographically stronger than a shorter complex password and significantly more memorable.

5. Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

5.1 MFA Requirements by Account Type

Multi-factor authentication is mandatory for the following access scenarios without exception: all privileged and administrative account access; all remote access including VPN connections; all access to email systems; all access to cloud-hosted services; all access to financial or HR systems; all access to source code repositories; any access from unmanaged or personal devices.

5.2 Approved MFA Methods

MFA implementations shall use NIST AAL2-compliant authenticators. Methods are listed in order of preference:

  1. Hardware Security Keys (FIDO2/WebAuthn): YubiKey or equivalent — strongest option; phishing-resistant
  2. Time-Based One-Time Password (TOTP): Microsoft Authenticator, Google Authenticator, Authy — strong option
  3. Push Notification: Microsoft Authenticator push approval — acceptable
  4. SMS/Voice OTP: Acceptable only where no stronger option is available; subject to SIM-swap risk
SMS-based MFA is the weakest approved form and is subject to SIM-swapping attacks. Its use should be limited and replaced with stronger options where feasible. Users must report lost or stolen MFA devices to IT immediately.

6. Password Storage and Transmission

  • Passwords shall never be stored in plaintext. Systems must store passwords using approved one-way hashing algorithms (bcrypt, Argon2, or PBKDF2 with minimum 100,000 iterations)
  • Passwords shall never be transmitted over unencrypted channels. All authentication must occur over TLS 1.2 or higher
  • Passwords shall never be stored in documents, spreadsheets, email, chat applications, or browser storage
  • Organizational password managers (Bitwarden Enterprise or equivalent) shall be used for credential storage
  • Passwords shall never be shared verbally, via email, or in any written form

7. Account Lockout

Account lockout policies shall be enforced on all systems to limit brute-force attack effectiveness:

  • Standard accounts: lock after 5 consecutive failed attempts; unlock automatically after 30 minutes
  • Privileged accounts: lock after 3 consecutive failed attempts; require administrator unlock
  • All lockout events shall be logged and reviewed daily for patterns
📁 Lab Evidence: Brute-force detection and automated alerting implemented in brute_detector.py — logs 847 failed SSH attempts in 10 minutes and triggers alert (INC-002). GPO account lockout policy enforced across domain as documented in gpo_audit.txt. Source: Foundations_Lab_Final/week-03

8. Session Management

  • Workstation session lock: activates after 15 minutes of inactivity on all managed workstations
  • Application session timeout: web applications shall terminate sessions after 30 minutes of inactivity
  • Remote sessions: VPN sessions terminate after 8 hours and after 30 minutes of inactivity
  • Concurrent sessions: privileged accounts limited to 1 active session; standard accounts limited to 3

9. Violations

Sharing credentials, using another user's credentials, storing passwords in unauthorized locations, or circumventing authentication requirements constitutes a policy violation subject to disciplinary action. If credential compromise is suspected, the user must report immediately — delayed reporting that results in expanded impact will be considered an aggravating factor.
