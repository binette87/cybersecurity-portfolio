1. Purpose

This policy establishes the organizational framework for managing information security incidents from initial detection through post-incident review. It defines roles, responsibilities, procedures, and timelines to ensure that security incidents are handled in a consistent, documented, and effective manner that minimizes impact to organizational operations and protects evidentiary integrity.

This policy is implemented in accordance with NIST SP 800-61 Rev 2, Computer Security Incident Handling Guide.

2. Scope

This policy applies to all security incidents — or suspected incidents — affecting organizational information systems, data, personnel, or operations. It applies to all employees, contractors, and third parties with access to organizational systems, who share the responsibility to detect and report potential incidents promptly.

3. Governing NIST Controls

IR-4
Incident Handling: The organization implements an incident handling capability for security incidents that includes preparation, detection and analysis, containment, eradication, and recovery. The organization coordinates incident handling activities with contingency planning activities.
IR-5
Incident Monitoring: The organization tracks and documents information system security incidents. All incidents are assigned unique identifiers, categorized by severity, and tracked through resolution in the incident register.
IR-6
Incident Reporting: The organization requires personnel to report suspected security incidents to the organizational incident response capability within defined timeframes. The organization reports information about incidents to designated external authorities as required.
IR-8
Incident Response Plan: The organization develops an incident response plan that provides the organization with a roadmap for implementing its incident response capability. The plan is reviewed and updated at minimum annually and following any major incident.

4. Incident Classification

All security incidents shall be classified by severity upon initial reporting. Classification determines response timelines and escalation requirements.

Critical Active breach with confirmed data exfiltration, ransomware, or complete system compromise Active ransomware encryption; confirmed data theft; system takeover 15 minutes
High Significant security event with material impact or high potential for escalation Unauthorized privileged access; malware detected; credential compromise 1 hour
Medium Security event with limited current impact but requiring investigation Brute-force attacks; policy violations; suspicious account activity 4 hours

5. Incident Response Lifecycle

The organization follows the NIST SP 800-61 Rev 2 incident response lifecycle consisting of six phases:

1
Preparation
Maintain IR capability, tools, contacts, and playbooks. Conduct annual tabletop exercises. Ensure all personnel have completed IR awareness training. Maintain current asset inventory and network documentation.
2
Detection & Analysis
Monitor systems for indicators of compromise via IDS, SIEM, endpoint monitoring, and user reports. Validate and triage reported events. Assign initial severity classification. Assign incident handler and open incident ticket.
3
Containment
Isolate affected systems from the network. Preserve system state — do not power off affected systems unless directed. Implement short-term containment to prevent spread. Document all containment actions with timestamps.
4
Eradication
Identify and eliminate the root cause. Remove malware, unauthorized accounts, or malicious code. Patch or remediate the vulnerability that was exploited. Verify eradication through forensic analysis before proceeding.
5
Recovery
Restore affected systems from clean backups or rebuild from known-good state. Validate system integrity before returning to production. Monitor closely for 72 hours post-recovery for signs of recurrence.
6
Post-Incident Activity
Conduct post-mortem within 5 business days. Document timeline, root cause, impact, actions taken, and lessons learned. Update IR playbooks, detection rules, and controls based on findings. Brief leadership on significant incidents.

6. Roles and Responsibilities

  • Incident Handler: Leads the response to assigned incidents; coordinates containment, eradication, and recovery activities; documents all actions
  • Security Analyst: Performs technical analysis, forensics, and artifact collection; maintains chain-of-custody for all evidence
  • System Owner: Authorizes containment and recovery actions for systems under their authority; participates in post-incident review
  • All Personnel: Responsible for reporting suspected incidents promptly; prohibited from independently investigating or attempting to remediate
  • Legal/HR: Consulted for incidents involving potential criminal activity, employee conduct, or regulatory reporting requirements

7. Evidence Handling and Chain of Custody

All evidence collected during incident response shall be handled in accordance with chain-of-custody procedures. Evidence must be documented with: item description, collection date/time, collector identity, storage location, and handling history. Evidence shall not be altered, deleted, or transferred without documented authorization. Forensic copies shall be made before analysis of original media.

8. External Reporting Requirements

Certain incidents may require external notification to regulatory authorities, law enforcement, or affected individuals. External reporting decisions shall be made in consultation with legal counsel and executive leadership.

  • Data breaches involving personal information: notify affected individuals per applicable privacy law
  • Incidents involving criminal activity: coordinate with law enforcement as appropriate
  • Incidents affecting critical infrastructure: report to CISA and applicable sector-specific agencies
  • All external communications regarding incidents require executive approval

9. Testing and Maintenance

The incident response plan shall be tested at minimum annually through tabletop exercises simulating realistic threat scenarios. Results of testing shall be documented and used to update the plan. The incident response capability shall also be evaluated following any significant real incident.
