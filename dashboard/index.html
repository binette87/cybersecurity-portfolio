Module 1 — Phishing Awareness
Recognize and respond to social engineering attacks
✓ Complete ▾
Slide 1 of 4
What Is Phishing?
Phishing is a social engineering attack in which a threat actor impersonates a trusted entity to deceive individuals into revealing sensitive information, credentials, or executing malicious actions.
🎭 Impersonation
🔗 Malicious Links
📎 Infected Attachments
⏰ Urgency Tactics
Slide 2 of 4
Red Flags to Identify Phishing
Train yourself to scrutinize every email before acting. The following indicators are commonly present in phishing attempts:
  • Sender domain does not match the organization's official domain (e.g., support@micros0ft-help.com)
  • Urgent language demanding immediate action ("Your account will be suspended in 24 hours")
  • Generic salutations such as "Dear User" or "Dear Customer"
  • Mismatched or obfuscated hyperlinks — hover before clicking
  • Requests for credentials, payment, or sensitive data via email
Slide 3 of 4
Anatomy of a Phishing Email
Examine this simulated phishing example. Highlighted elements indicate indicators of compromise:
From: IT-Security@corp-helpdesk-portal.net
Subject: ⚠ URGENT: Account Verification Required

Dear Employee,

Your account will be permanently disabled unless you verify
your credentials within 2 hours.

Click here: http://bit.ly/xK92pQ

— Legitimate IT will NEVER ask for your password via email —
Slide 4 of 4
Your Correct Response Protocol
When you suspect a phishing attempt, follow this response sequence without exception:
  • Do not click any links or open attachments
  • Do not reply to or forward the email
  • Report the email to your security team immediately using the designated reporting mechanism
  • If credentials were entered, report immediately and initiate a password reset
  • Preserve the original email as evidence — do not delete

A. Reply with your password — IT needs it to fix the issue
B. Click the link provided to verify your account on a secure portal
C. Do not engage. Report the email to the security team and delete it
D. Forward it to colleagues to warn them

Module 2 — Password Hygiene & MFA
Credential security and multi-factor authentication
✓ Complete ▾
Slide 1 of 4
Why Credentials Are the Primary Target
According to the Verizon Data Breach Investigations Report, over 80% of hacking-related breaches involve compromised or weak credentials. Your password is the first and most critical line of defense.
80%+ breaches use stolen creds
Credential stuffing attacks
Password reuse exploitation
Slide 2 of 4
Characteristics of a Strong Password
A strong password is one that is computationally infeasible to crack and unique across all accounts:
Password123 ✗
8 chars, predictable
P@$$w0rd! ✗
Common substitution
Tr0ub4dor&3 ✓
Long, complex, unique
correct-horse-battery ✓
Passphrase — long & memorable
  • Minimum 16 characters in length
  • Unique per account — never reuse
  • Managed via a password manager (Bitwarden, 1Password)
Slide 3 of 4
Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
MFA requires a second verification factor beyond your password. Even if credentials are compromised, MFA blocks unauthorized access in 99.9% of cases (Microsoft, 2019).
  • Something you know — password or PIN
  • Something you have — authenticator app (TOTP), hardware key (YubiKey)
  • Something you are — biometric (fingerprint, face ID)
  • SMS-based MFA is the weakest form — SIM swapping is a known attack vector. Use authenticator apps when possible.
Slide 4 of 4
Password Management Best Practices
Follow these organizational standards to maintain credential integrity:
  • Use a password manager — never store passwords in browsers or plaintext files
  • Enable MFA on all accounts, especially email, VPN, and administrative systems
  • Change passwords immediately upon suspected compromise — do not wait
  • Never share credentials with colleagues, including IT support staff
  • Never use corporate passwords for personal accounts

C. The password may be too long for some systems

Module 3 — Incident Reporting
Recognize, classify, and escalate security incidents
✓ Complete ▾
Slide 1 of 4
What Constitutes a Security Incident?
A security incident is any event that threatens the confidentiality, integrity, or availability of organizational information systems or data. All employees have a responsibility to identify and report suspicious activity.
Unauthorized access
Data exfiltration
Malware infection
Policy violations
System anomalies
Slide 2 of 4
Common Incident Indicators
The following behavioral and technical indicators should trigger an immediate report to your security team:
  • Unusual system slowness, crashes, or unresponsive applications
  • Unexpected account lockouts or password change notifications
  • Unfamiliar programs running or files appearing without explanation
  • Abnormal outbound network traffic or data transfers
  • Colleagues reporting they received suspicious emails from your account
  • Missing or altered files, especially sensitive documents
Slide 3 of 4
The Incident Reporting Process
Time is critical in incident response. Follow this reporting sequence to minimize organizational impact:
  • Step 1 — Contain: Disconnect the affected system from the network if safe to do so. Do not power off.
  • Step 2 — Preserve: Do not delete files, clear logs, or modify the system. Preserve evidence.
  • Step 3 — Report: Contact the security team immediately via designated channel. Do not use the affected system.
  • Step 4 — Document: Record what you observed, when you observed it, and any actions you took.
Slide 4 of 4
Escalation Chain & Timing
Incident severity determines the escalation path. Early reporting — even for low-severity events — enables faster containment and reduces organizational impact significantly.
Critical — Report immediately (0–15 min)
High — Report within 1 hour
Medium — Report within 4 hours
Low — Report within 24 hours
When in doubt — report. There is no penalty for over-reporting a security event.

Module 4 — Insider Threat Awareness
Recognize, prevent, and report insider risk
✓ Complete ▾
Slide 1 of 4
Defining the Insider Threat
An insider threat originates from individuals with authorized access to organizational systems — including employees, contractors, and vendors. Unlike external threats, insiders bypass perimeter controls by design.
Malicious Insider — intentional data theft or sabotage
Negligent Insider — accidental policy violations or data exposure
Compromised Insider — legitimate account used by an external attacker
Slide 2 of 4
Behavioral Warning Signs
The following behavioral patterns have been correlated with insider threat activity across CISA and CERT research. None are definitive on their own, but in combination they warrant attention:
  • Accessing systems or data outside of normal working hours without business justification
  • Downloading or transferring large volumes of data, especially to personal storage
  • Expressing grievance about the organization or discussing resignation while increasing data access
  • Attempting to access resources beyond their role or job function
  • Disabling or circumventing security controls on their workstation
Slide 3 of 4
Protecting Sensitive Data
Your role in preventing insider threats is active, not passive. Apply these principles in your daily work:
  • Access only the data your role requires — do not request broader access than necessary
  • Lock your workstation when stepping away — even briefly
  • Never copy sensitive data to personal devices, email, or cloud storage without authorization
  • Properly dispose of sensitive documents — shred physical copies, securely delete digital files
  • Report colleague behavior that raises concern through the appropriate confidential channel
Slide 4 of 4
Your Responsibility & Reporting
You are not a surveillance officer — but you are an active participant in organizational security culture. Reporting suspicious behavior is a professional responsibility, not a personal accusation.
  • Report concerns through the designated confidential reporting channel
  • Do not confront suspected insiders directly — escalate to security or HR
  • Document specific behaviors with timestamps, not general impressions
  • Understand that organizational policies protect both the organization and employees
All reports are treated as confidential. Retaliation against good-faith reporters is strictly prohibited.
